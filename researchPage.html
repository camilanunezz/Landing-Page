Predictive Modeling of Depth Thermography

Predicting temperature distributions beneath the surface of objects is of high interest for a variety of science and engineering applications. Here, we develop a machine learning model built from a technique called depth thermography, based on infrared thermal spectrum data that can remotely determine volumetric temperature distributions. Currently, depth thermography uses physical equations to make its predictionsâ€”however, these equations are highly noise-sensitive. To solve this problem, we created a feedforward neural network that achieves a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 3.53 Kelvin, a significant improvement over the equation's predictions, with noisy input data. In addition, two other network approaches were created and tested. Of the three, the feedforward neural network proved to have both the highest accuracy and efficiency. This model could give depth thermography a novel ability to make volumetric temperature predictions for a wide assortment of objects, ranging from multilayer semiconductor devices to volumes of liquids and gasses.

The original research on depth thermography was conducted by Dr. Yuzhe Xiao, Assistant Physics Professor at the University of North Texas, click here to read further on the background of Dr. Xiao's work. The research we built upon began with an MAE of 13 Kelvin when calculating 3-D temperatures. With the advanced applications of machine learning, my team and I reduced this value to an increase in accuracy from 13.0 Kelvin to 3.53 based on a trained model.

The primary skills applied to this work include Machine Learning, Python, and Pytorch. Click this link to view the GitHub Repository . This research was funded by the National Science Foundation (NSF) in 2023.
